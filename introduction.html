A Brief History of the Atom

The “Billiard Ball” Model

One of the most important contributors to chemistry is John Dalton, who reintroduced the theory of the atom in the early 1800’s. He proposed that it was the main component of matter and envisioned them as hard sphere-like particles.

The “Plum Pudding” Model

The next progression in the model of the atom came from J.J. Thompson, who proposed that the atom was actually made up of a positively charged sphere which had negatively charged particles inside of it. This theory proposed the existence of sub-atomic particles.

The “Rutherford” model

The next change in the model of the atom came from Ernest Rutherford, who discovered that the atom was mostly made up of empty space in his famous gold foil experiment. In Rutherford’s experiment, alpha particles were shot at a thin metal foil. These particles would then either be deflected by the foil or pass through it. The results showed that most of the particles were not deflected by the foil, proving that the atom mostly consisted of empty space.

The “Bohr” Model

Niels Bohr's model of the atom theorized that electrostatic (coulombic) attraction kept the electrons surrounding the nucleus in orbit. These electrons were at their lowest energy state when they were closest to the nucleus, and electrostatic repulsion kept electrons from getting too near to each other. The Bohr model of the atom is important because it correctly describes the nature of the energy of electrons in the atom. Bohr realized that electrons on the outer shells of atoms were higher energy, which were less favorable and therefore less stable. Although this theory is not completely accurate, it was still a major contributor to the modern theory of the atom.

The Electron “Cloud” Model

The most current model of the atom is an updated version of the Bohr model known as the Electron Cloud model. This model is more accurate, since it predicts the most probable location of electron density in an atom.
